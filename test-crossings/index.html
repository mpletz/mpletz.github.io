Chapter 2: Modeling rolling contact fatigue in crossing noses

In the railway track, turnouts (also called points or switches) let the train switch from one track to another. They are therefore a key part of the track structure, particularly close to and at railway stations. The crossings consist of a switch panel (where the switch rails guide the train either on one or the other track) and a crossing panel, where the inner rails of the two tracks cross each other. There are three crossing solutions where a) the wheel flange just runs over the continuous rails that are in the way, b) the wheel flange runs through slits in the fixed rails, dividing the rails into a crossing nose (or frog) and two wing rails, or c) the setup is similar to (b), but with a movable crossing nose or movable wing rails to obtain a continuous running surface. A fixed crossing panel (b) with its wing rails and crossing nose is shown in Fig. 1a.

As one wheelset of a train runs over the fixed crossing, the contact position on the wheel profile laterally changes. facing/trailing move (those words are used by @kassa-2008). facing run, as illustrated in Fig. 1: typical intermediate position on the regular rail (point A in Fig. 1) . then shifted away from flange in the diverging wing rail (towards point B). Impact on the crossing nose close to the wheel flange (point C). As continues to run, the lateral contact position shift backwards to point A. trailing route: the same procedure, but backwards.

Plastic load redistribution in the crossing nose

Repeated impact of wheels on the narrow crossing nose: plastic deformation that flattens the top and therefore increases the size of the contact surface, as described by Johnson @johnson-1989, who states that three mechanisms contribute to the shakedown process:

  1. residual stresses through plastic deformation,
  2. strain hardening of the material, and
  3. flattening of the surfaces due to plastic deformation.

Besides the issue that hundreds of cycles of this system need to be computed to accurately capture this “geometric shakedown” (as described in @johnson-1989), the crossing is not overrun each time by the same wheel profile. On the contrary, there can be a wide rage of worn wheel profiles, that influence the position of impact on the crossing nose. These profiles can flatten the crossing nose in different directions, which limits the reduction of contact pressure for each of these wheels.

Simplified model of crossing impact: get angle, WR radius, and CN radius from geometry, then compute the impact for that. More general applicable and faster to run.

To study the distribution of wheel profiles and multiple load cycles in reasonable computation times, a simplified model for the impact of the wheel on the crossing nose has been developed. It introduces a wheel with a flat running surface, and reduces the crossing nose to one where the head radius and the vertical level are both defined as a function of the longitudinal coordinate xx. Furthermore, there are no wing rails in this simplified model. Instead, a geometrical analysis predicts the position xx and the angle φ\varphi that the wheel impacts on the crossing nose, see Fig. 3. In this way, a range of wheel profiles can easily be modeled, and computation times can be considerably reduced. The dynamics of the wheel on the wing rail are therefore disregarded, which is a good approximation for not too big train speeds.

This simple model is introduced in Paper A1. describe in more detail… only linear elastic, disregarding the slip, check in which cases the assumption of the wheel just following the wing rail profile is valid, and a parameter study with varied train speed, impact angle, and crossing nose radius (which is constant with respect to xx). Results of vertical impact forces and the maximum contact stresses.

Mention that by design, this simplified model does not account for slip introduced by the discrepancy of the rolling radius between the wheel running on the wing rail and running on the crossing nose. For the plastic deformation and the denting of the crossing nose, this slip can be disregarded. When assessing wear or surface cracking, however, it is crucial to incorporate the slip. Therefore, the model has been extended to also account for the slipping @wiedorn-2019a.

Multiple load cycles

The crossing node plastically adapts to the loading in the first dozens of load cycles and then only continues to plastically deform in a very small (unwanted) extend or if completely different wheels come along. Therefore, the plastic adaption of the crossing nose to the loading can be studied with a limited number of load cycles. The simple model proposed in Paper A is taken and overrun by a) one wheel profile or b) a range of wheel profiles, which repeatedly impact onto the crossing nose, as indicated in Figure 5. If the same wheel impacts onto the crossing nose, it makes a distinctive dent in the crossing nose, which can in turn lead to increased dynamic forces. This increase in dynamic forces can increase the loads and then lead to further plastic deformation, or even to issues regarding the run of the whole train, which would require re-profiling of the crossing nose. For the varied wheel profiles, the dent is more smooth and issues with increased dynamic forces are limited.

This plastic adaption to the wheel profile spectrum, which the crossing is exposed to, contributes to the durability of the crossing. It is the reason for using high Manganese steels (also called Hadfileld steels, with a typical Manganese content of 13% @liu-2011) that initially have a small yield stress of about 300 MPa, but work harden extensively, with an increase in the yield stress of way above 1000 MPa. This means that they initially change their geometry but then tend to stabilize due to the geometric adaption, buildup of residual stresses, and hardening of the material.

Issues of manganese steel for high cycle numbers

Two stages can be defined in the life of a crossing nose: a) the run-in process in the first few cycles and b) the further cyclic plastic deformation of the run-in geometry, as shown in Figure 6.

  1. In the run-in process, the plastic deformation should be large enough to reduce the stresses, but not so large that the impact forces are considerably increased. A constitutive model of the crossing material needs to accurately represent these first cycles.
  2. The further plastic deformation is more subtle: it is interesting, if there is further and further unfavorable plastic deformation over thousands of cycles. Which either needs constitutive models that can accurately account for the ratcheting of the material, or simple approaches that judge if shakedown is reached based on stresses computed in the model and the yield stress in the material. The shakedown maps are widely used to do so, which plot the maximum contact pressure divided by the shear yield stress over the creepage

This plastic adaption also has three practical effects: a) the tolerances in the initial geometry are not so crucial as for high-strength steels. Indeed, it could even make sense to account for the plastic deformation and optimize the initial geometry so to obtain a preferable run-in geometry. b) If a very worn wheel profile comes along, the impact rather leads to plastic deformation and adapts to it, but c) the low initial yield stress is associated with a low hardness, which lies below the requirements regarding the track infrastructure. Since Manganese crossings are widely used, there obviously has been a solution for this too small hardness.

But how to increase the hardness on the running surfaces of a cast Manganese crossing? For continuous profiles, cold working such as rolling is widely used. It introduces plastic deformations and residual stresses and therefore increases the hardness. But somehow rolling a crossing may be possible, but can be very tedious considering the many different crossing geometries, and that after rolling, the crossing should have a defined geometry. So the solution that crossing manufacturers have come up with is explosive hardening: some layers of explosives are stacked on the cast crossings. During the explosion, the shock wave introduces plastic deformations in the top layer of the crossing @zhang-2010 @liu-2011, obtaining acceptable hardness.

The damage assessment toolbox

The geometry of the crossing and the wheel are of course crucial for the contact points and dynamic forces. But as a next step, the question is how the model results can be used to assess damage. Evaluating damage variables from the following stress and strain fields in the model can be too complicated. Therefore, in Paper D we came up with up with an interface using a limited number of load parameters of the contact patch evaluated in the full FEM model that are used in an input in existing, and, at a later point, can be used in newly developed damage models.

These load parameters of the contact patch are the maximum contact pressure p0p_0, the creepage cc, and the contact patch length aa. For the damage evaluation, furthermore the train speed vv and coefficient of friction μ\mu are needed.

This means that for each longitudinal position of the wheel, these parameters can be evaluated and used to predict damage separately for the wing rail and the crossing nose. It therefore allows the identify critical regions in crossings.

To first show how this could work, existing damage criteria are used in this setup. The FEM model of one crossing nose is build up, load parameters are evaluated and then they are used to assess wear with a wear map, RCF with a shakedown map, and subsurface damage with the Dang-Van criterion.

Both RCF damage and subsurface damage can be evaluated based onp0p_0 and cc, only. From the shear yield strength kk and the Dang Van parameters, borders of the damage / no damage regions can be drawn in the p0p_0-cc diagram. Then, the points along the longitudinal position along the wing rail and crossing nose can be plotted in the same diagram, indicating positions of damage.

For predicting wear, first the wear regime needs to be identified in the p0p_0-cc diagram. Then, Archard’s wear law @archard-1953 can predict the wear depth using the maximum contact pressure p0p_0 and the total sliding distance as the contact patch crosses one point of the crossing, which corresponds to 2 a c2\,a\,c. wear map used from @jendel-2002 and discussed in @lewis-2004.

Figure 8 shows how the damage assessment toolbox can be used to evaluate arising damage along the longitudinal coordinate of a crossing nose. It identifies the extend of wear in the different regions as well as the regions where surface RCF and subsurface damage are expected. In Paper D, these predicted regions are compared to observations from the field, and show a qualitative agreement.
