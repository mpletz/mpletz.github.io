Chapter 3: A closer look at cracks and wear in the wheel/rail contact

Modern railway infrastructure heavily depends on the function of the rails. In recent years, there is a trend to increased traffic on rails, higher velocities, and higher axle loads. Keeping up with these demands in the development of better steels or rail manufacturing is a huge challenge. The key to such innovations is identifying damage mechanisms in the wheel/rail contact and explaining them using principles from engineering mechanics and material science.

Modeling cyclic plastic deformation

The first step towards predicting the onset of surface cracks and wear is to model the cyclic plastic deformation in rails. The obvious way to do is to repeatedly roll a wheel over a part that represents a rail, as shown for a 2D case in Figure 3. This was studied in Paper G3. The wheel can be either a part with contact modeled to the rail surface or just a contact patch with a contact pressure pp- and traction τ\tau distribution. With such a model, as introduced in Paper G, the plastic shear strain γpl\gamma_\mathrm{pl} and the shear displacement uxu_\mathrm{x} are computed. Both increase towards the rail surface and with increasing load cycles.

(frage, warum es immer weiter geht?!?)

As the plastic shear strain increases, the rail‘s grains get more and more aligned and refined. As a simple approach, one can define a critical γpl\gamma_\mathrm{pl} where the fracture toughness perpendicular to the shear lines plotted in Figure 3 becomes practically 0 as γpl,c\gamma_\mathrm{pl,c}. One can then assume that into a depth where γpl>γpl,c\gamma_\mathrm{pl} > \gamma_\mathrm{pl,c}, the material is already full of small cracks that follow the shear lines. They can either be regarded as wear (assuming that this whole layer crumbles and falls of the rail, see paper on wear debris) or as a bunch of starting cracks, that can continue to grow and may become dangerous considering rail break.

Such cyclic rollover models with hundreds of cycles, particularly if they consider the 3D geometry of the rail, are computationally very costly. Furthermore, they have a position where the wheel or contact patch is placed on the rail and one where it is lifted off the rail. These two regions are prone to artifacts concerning the plastic strain field. So the rolling distance must bo long enough so that a steady state rolling and plastic strain field is reached in the middle, increasing the computation time of the models. And this necessary length even depends on the number of cycles and is not known before running the simulation, which makes it even more complicated. Getting rid of these regions where the wheel starts and stops rolling on the rail, would make the plastic deformation more uniform and reduce the rolling length necessary for the cyclic model.

Knut Andreas Mayer came up with an idea to get rid of these regions using a special kind of periodic boundary conditions, sketched in Figure 4. It involves periodic boundary conditions of the starting and end surface of the rail. For the wheel to run onto the left side of the rail as it would fall off the right side, he introduced shadow elements that extend the running surface outside the rail and are coupled to the other side of the rail. The wheel runs a distance LL in each cycle, which corresponds to the length of the solid part of the rail.

This model therefore corresponds to an infinite number of wheels with a distance LL running on a rail. The only limitation is that the rail is never fully unloaded, and that for small LL values, the stress fields of the wheels may interact and lead to increased loads and an overestimation of plastic strains. The running length LL, however, can be considerably smaller than in a classical model, which makes the model much faster. Surface roughness and cracks in rails

Surfaces are almost never flat, particularly if one looks close enough. For materials with such high stiffness as steel (compared to rubber tires), this significantly influents the stress fields, and models for plastic deformations close to rail surfaces should not fully disregard surface roughness.

initial roughness —> influences first pl. deformations, but once wear and crack initiation sets in (that could be predicted as described in Paper D), the roughness will be influenced by tiny breakouts. Therefore, there will be a run-in roughness of the rail surface that may be independent on the initial roughness and is rather influenced by the material properties and the tribological properties of the contact.

Nevertheless, in Paper H4, the initial surface roughness of a wheel and a rail were modeled in a 2D rolling model, see Figure 5. The influence of the roughness on the plastic shear strain close to the surface was identified and it was shown that on top of the γpl\gamma_\mathrm{pl} curve that increases towards the surface, there is an additional contribution very close to the surface due to the roughness.

To make the conclusions drawn for this roughness model applicable also to other rough surfaces, the crucial roughness parameters for γpl\gamma_\mathrm{pl} were identified. To do so, the roughness profiles were reduced to a characteristic amplitude AA and wavelength λ\lambda, and it was found that γpl\gamma_\mathrm{pl} is proportional to A/λA/\lambda.

Toni Arbeit: shown, how the vast plastic deformation affects the microstructure: grain refinement, but mainly alignment of the elongated grains: Kic becomes very small in one direction but stays at the initial level in the other directions. Crucial for wear and onset of bigger cracks in rail surfaces.

Such a local microstructure with complex fracture behavior such as anisotropic fracture toughness can also be produced on purpose, not only by a certain grain structure but also by using multiple phases to obtain a heterogeneous material. Such heterogeneous materials with interesting fracture mechanisms are widely found in natural materials (zitate), but also in artificial materials such as the block-copolymer Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene (ABS). Unterstanding these damage mechanisms using numerical models is an interesting endeavor, and the following chapter presents my attempts to contribute to it.
